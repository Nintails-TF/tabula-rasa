# 1. Constructing a Leadership Identity:

It's difficult to pin down your own identity and your own characteristics of yourself. You've got a personality, values, ideas, and will continue to evolve in the future as well. 

It's key since it dictates how you interact with others, what you prioritise, what others expect, and what you expect from others, and how your relationships operate.

 [[../Glossary#^Identity-def|Identity]] can be defined in various ways. Multiple disciplines such as political, social science, humanities, business studies, philosophy, psychology, have covered the idea of identity - each having their own angle and perspective, the more you learn the more you realise the deep layers and more material there is.

Another idea, is that **the actions you take bring you towards or further away from certain identities**. Studying brings you together with other university students on your course - as you work together and experience struggles/wins. Or more broadly students as a whole. But it also could bring you away from your family or work - as you choose to spend time studying rather than being with them.

It is always important to consider: **who do I want to be?**

## 1.2. Multiple Identities:

Your identity shifts depending on who you are talking to: Speaking to your Dad -> Son, Speaking to Classmate -> Pupil, Speaking at work -> Colleagues/Managers/Administrators/Leaders.

There are certain traits or things you would talk about with a friend whilst at the pub, and there is a way in which you would act at work or during a meeting. Since different audiences value and reward different behaviours.

You want to have a stable core or values, but to express them differently in changing environments, this means that your expression is real and isn't performative, which means people will actually believe and trust you more and you won't deal with psychological turbulence.

# 2. Leader, Manager or Administrator?:

Leaders within turbulent environments can be morphed or convinced to become managers or administrators. [The Quinn Mills (2005)](https://books.google.co.uk/books/about/Principles_of_Management.html?id=237vAAAACAAJ&redir_esc=y)) the paper we are exploring here and his concepts.

[[../Glossary#^Admin-def|Administrators]] vary on the field/industry and organisations size. Smaller organisations use admins to acts as receptionists (Get appointments in order), secretaries (Get leaders/key figures in order), office managers (Get the office running in order), IT support (Ensure IT systems run smoothly and run according to policy/best practices), etc. Larger organisations use admins to collaborate with teams, oversee operations, and manage departments, etc.

[[../Glossary#^Manager-def|Managers]] make and execute plans. They assign what tasks need to be done, supervise people to ensure they are done, ensure money/resources aren't wasted, and resolve problems that arise. Managers are different from admins, as they provide the direction for the team rather than the infrastructure. Admins support the system, managers support the output.

### 1. What Role Does She Fulfil?:
Natalia is an **administrator.** She both lacks in managerial (Poor organisation and staffing, weak controlling, managers feel unable to solve problems) an leadership qualities (No inspiring vision, pessimistic demoralises staff, lack of stability, poor adaptation due to frequent travelling.) The main idea is that she is struggling and can process and report information upwards but fails to manage systems or lead people.

### 2. How Can She Use More Elements of Leadership?:

**Communication:**
*Replace extreme pessimism/optimism with transparent and balanced updates. Frame challenges as opportunities (High turnover means we can grow or rebuild internally), have regular presence despite travel (Video calls, check-ins) or renegotiate travel.*

**Vision:**
*Have a clear mission statement to navigate the transition period. Co-create a plan with Christine and Ivan, celebrate small wins to build momentum in hard times.*

**Empowerment:**
*Give Christine and Ivan decision making authority during her absences, shield them from unnecessary pessimism whilst keeping them informed on the more executive leadership. Recognise their ability to problem solve rather than bottlenecking the process.*

**Motivation via action:**
*Own the budget failure rather than blame game, demonstrate accountability, show investment in the team as people - not just the company.*

### 3. What Advice Would You Give Her?:

Don't manage upward as much, manage downwards and support your teams and people rather than supporting the top as much. Managers and staff are the foundation of the company and are required to be in good shape to function.

1. Stop blame game - own the failure and communicate with Christine and Ivan
2. Close the budget gap - work with finance and fix the problem at hand.
3. Change communication tone - Not emotional: replace doom with "this is the situation, here's our plan."
