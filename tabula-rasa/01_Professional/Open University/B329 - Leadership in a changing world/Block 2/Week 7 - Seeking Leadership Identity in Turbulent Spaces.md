# 2. Leader, Manager or Administrator?:

[[../Glossary#^Leader-def|Leaders]] within turbulent environments can be morphed or convinced to become managers or administrators. [The Quinn Mills (2005)](https://books.google.co.uk/books/about/Principles_of_Management.html?id=237vAAAACAAJ&redir_esc=y)) the paper we are exploring here and his concepts.

[[../Glossary#^Admin-def|Administrators]] vary on the field/industry and organisations size. Smaller organisations use admins to acts as receptionists (Get appointments in order), secretaries (Get leaders/key figures in order), office managers (Get the office running in order), IT support (Ensure IT systems run smoothly and run according to policy/best practices), etc. Larger organisations use admins to collaborate with teams, oversee operations, and manage departments, etc.

[[../Glossary#^Manager-def|Managers]] make and execute plans. They assign what tasks need to be done, supervise people to ensure they are done, ensure money/resources aren't wasted, and resolve problems that arise. Managers are different from admins, as they provide the direction for the team rather than the infrastructure. Admins support the system, managers support the output.

# 3. Becoming an Inspiring Leader:

Where as block 1 introduced the idea of **Charismatic Leaders**. We're understanding that they use that approach through personality and unique appearances for others to follow them, we can apply this logic to inspiration.

## Activity 7 - Becoming an Inspiring Leader:

### 1. What Can People Do to Create an Inspiring Identity?:

The idea is that you as an inspiring leader want to:
- Make people feel heard and understood.
	- Build credibility and trust within people.
- Give meaning to the work (The "why").
	- Reveal or show the impact of work beyond that task that a person is doing.
- Set the direction and the course of the ship.
	- Develop a vision or idea and understand it deeply.
- Create a belief that the destination is worth working towards.
	- Articulate your vision clearly to other people, talk in a language they understand.
- Motivates action through incentives other than authority or money.
	- Give autonomy/freedom, mastery, purpose, belonging, recognition/pride, etc.
		- Different people have different values, so you'd want to tune it to the individual.
### 2. Role Model and Antithesis of Inspiring Leaders:

Personally, I think **Charlie Munger** is a good role model of an inspiring leader. He's stable and consistent and compounded growth over time rather than marching people towards a machine gun. His vision was "Invert, always invert" and about avoiding stupidity rather than chasing brilliance, which can work very well within an environment such as the stock market and financial markets, as they reward the analysis and understanding of risk - be it a high or low risk approach.

He also acted and treated other's in a simple and straightforward way, though the he could be very abrasive and dismissive sometimes as well. But he and Buffet cultivated success and chose great people to work at Berkshire Hathaway.

I'm against the mythology of grandiose start-ups and people who are visionaries. Yes, there are people who have an idea that has merit, but it also can enable toxic and destructive behaviours. Having a clear, well articulated vision is great and being inspirational is also great. But don't abuse that power.

**Elizabeth Homes** from Theranos is an example of inspiration gone wrong, she developed a vision and a followership, but instead of tackling the real problems at hand, she covered up the truth

## 3. What Makes an Inspiring Leadership Identity?:

Having a well-built and consistent moral framework that is built from experience and reality testing is key for inspiration within a leader. Then translating your core values into words that different audiences can understand. That way your "voice" comes from within and people can understand you.

For Munger, his values systems evolved from working 12 hour shifts in a greengrocer with Munger when he was a teenager, going and living through the great depression as well as his family, who ended up helping other and their community - They didn't try and extract as much wealth as possible, but acted with integrity and honesty. This in turn positively influenced his business ventures as he built connections and built positive habits.

For Homes, her vision of developing blood testing technology from a pinprick of blood for a wide range of tests was strong. But her value system was a faÃ§ade, it was an inauthentic representation of Steve Jobs. She lied to the public and investors through pretending that the **Edison** machine worked. She knew that the technology wasn't there and couldn't work, this trickled down into other managers who either deceived or controlled employees with anger and fear.

Holmes's vision *was* inspirational - genuinely transformative if achieved. The failure wasn't ambition; it was honesty. She knew the Edison couldn't deliver and deployed it anyway. A good vision executed through lies isn't leadership, it's fraud. Inspiration requires both a compelling destination *and* truthfulness about where you actually are on the journey.

It's good to be inspirational like Homes, but you've also got to be honest, open minded and have integrity - like Munger, because otherwise your vision can turn to delusion.

### 4. What Could You Do to Change Your Leadership Identity?

Kane provides a practical framework:

**Shift focus from outward to inward**: Stop asking "How do I get them to behave differently?" and start asking "How do I need to be behaving differently?"

**Practice the centring technique**: The three-step physical process (upright posture, expanded awareness circle, softening tension) provides a concrete tool for managing reactivity in the moment.

**Develop self-regulation capacity**: Roger learned to "catch himself in the act" when he started convincing and cajoling, then pause and physically recentre before proceeding.

**Integrate both clarity and connection**: The goal isn't to choose between being direct or being empathetic, but to cultivate the capacity for both simultaneously. Roger's eventual success came from saying "here's where I stand on this, and what do you think?"

**Move from reactive to responsive**: By managing the automatic fight/flight/freeze response through physical centring, leaders can act skilfully rather than habitually.

# Summary:

## Core Concept

Leadership identity is **fluid and context-dependent** - who you are as a leader shifts based on situation, audience, and environment. Effective leadership requires both a stable core of values and the flexibility to express them differently across contexts.

## Key Distinctions

**Three Organizational Roles:**
- **Administrator**: Implements rules, maintains systems, processes forms and records (infrastructure support)
- **Manager**: Plans, budgets, assigns tasks, supervises execution (directional support)
- **Leader**: Influences thinking, sets vision, inspires action, helps others see possibilities (transformational support)

## Critical Insights

**Identity is Co-Constructed:**
- Your self-perception alone is insufficient
- Others must also perceive you as a leader
- You have partial control through presentation (language, actions, framing)
- Some characteristics remain beyond your control (demographics)

**Inspiring Leadership Components:**
- Energizes people toward a compelling future vision
- Motivates through purpose beyond authority or money
- Embodies organizational values authentically
- Creates excitement about change rather than fear of it

## The Reconstruction Challenge

Leadership identity can be **deliberately changed** through three steps (Kane, 2015):
1. Awareness: Understand your current identity and how you built it
2. Action: Begin behaving in new ways aligned with desired identity
3. Embodiment: Sustain new habits long enough that others recognize genuine shift

**Warning**: Flexibility must not become performance. Translation of values across contexts is authentic; deception eventually cracks under pressure.
