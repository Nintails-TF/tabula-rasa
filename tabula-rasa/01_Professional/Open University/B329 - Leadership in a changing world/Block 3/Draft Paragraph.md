# B329 - Leadership Challenge Draft:

In May 2017, the WannaCry ransomware attack struck the NHS, affecting over a third of all trusts in England, cancelling an estimated 19,494 appointments, and forcing five accident and emergency departments to divert patients (NAO, 2018). The attack exploited known vulnerabilities that NHS Digital had already warned trusts to patch, yet out of the 88 trusts inspected for cyber-security readiness before the attack, none had passed (NAO, 2018). If not for Marcus Hutchins, an independent security researcher who activated a kill switch by registering a domain in the malware's code for $10.69, the damage would have been far worse (Cloudflare, 2021).  
  
Seven years later, the alarm was ignored. In June 2024, the Qilin ransomware group attacked Synnovis, a pathology provider serving major London NHS trusts, crippling almost all of its IT systems. Over 10,000 outpatient appointments were cancelled (NHS England, 2025), 400GB of patient data was published on the dark web, and the attack cost an estimated £32.7 million (Gabert-Doyon, 2025). In June 2025, King's College Hospital confirmed that the disruption had contributed to the death of a patient (Warren, 2025).  
  
These are not isolated incidents. They are symptoms of structural failure in how public sector institutions fund, staff, and govern cybersecurity.  
  
These failures are rooted in a persistent unwillingness to value "invisible" security. Currently, a massive pay disparity exists: public sector cyber roles average £45,000, while equivalent roles in finance or law command £75,000 (Coker, 2025). This creates a political deadlock where NHS boards hesitate to divert funds from clinical care toward IT infrastructure. Yet, by choosing to wait for catastrophe rather than investing in talent, contractors and specialists are brought in at market rates or above. This undermines long-term resilience and accountability, and denies the organisation dedicated experts with a deep understanding of the environment. NHS leaders must exercise adaptive leadership by confronting this reality openly, recognising cybersecurity not as a secondary administrative cost, but as a prerequisite for patient safety.
