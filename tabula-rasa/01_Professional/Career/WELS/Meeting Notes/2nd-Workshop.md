## Scenario 2: The Group Project Imbalance

In a group project, one member does most of the work while others contribute very little. The group plans to submit the project as is.

**Scenario 2 → Response 2** Rather than letting the imbalance continue, actively try to contribute more and support the person carrying the load — even if it's late in the process.

**Fairness**

***

## Scenario 3: The Citation Slip

You paraphrase a paragraph from a source but forget to include a citation. Later, you realize this after submission.

**Scenario 3 → Response 3** Email the marker or module lead quickly to flag the missing citation before it becomes a formal misconduct issue. Getting ahead of it changes the framing entirely.

**Accuracy**

***
## Scenario 4: The Pressure to Perform

A student is under pressure to achieve a high grade and considers using an online essay-writing service.

**Scenario 4 → Response 4** Don't use the service — not just because it might get caught, but because it establishes a pattern of shortcuts that compounds over time and eventually fails you.

**Honesty**

***
## Scenario 5: The Peer Review Challenge

You are asked to peer-review a classmate’s work. You notice several errors but worry that pointing them out might upset them.

**Scenario 5 → Response 5** Frame feedback around the rubric and the person's success rather than personal criticism. "According to the marking criteria, this section could be stronger by doing X" removes the personal sting.

**Respect**

***
## Scenario 6: Speaking Up About Misconduct

You are working on a group project and discover that one member has copied large sections of text from an online source without citing it. The submission deadline is tomorrow, and the group is under pressure to finish. You worry that raising the issue might cause conflict or delay the submission.

**Scenario 6 → Response 6** Remove the plagiarised content, convene the group, and fix it properly — even if that means requesting an extension. A late submission is far less damaging than a misconduct finding against the whole group.

**Courage**

# Improved Draft:

**Context** It's Week 5 of your module. You've been managing your studies well, but this week you come down with a bad illness and miss a key lab session. The lab involved collecting data you'll need for your upcoming report, due in 3 days. You haven't missed anything before.

**Challenge** Your friend, who attended the lab, messages you: _"Don't stress — just use my results, no one will know."_ They mean well, but you're now caught between missing a deadline and using data you didn't collect yourself.

**Choices**
- Use your friend's data and submit the report as normal
- Email your tutor or student support to explain your absence and ask about your options
- Ask your tutor directly whether using a peer's lab results is permissible before doing anything

_What would you do? What stops you from contacting your tutor? Is the friend's offer actually helpful, or does it create a bigger problem?_

**Contemplate**
- If you used the data and passed, what would you actually have learned?
- How might this shortcut affect you if the same lab skills come up again — in an exam, a future module, or at work?
- What's the worst realistic outcome of emailing your tutor honestly?
