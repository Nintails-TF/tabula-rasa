# Introduction:

You've got the 2010 Equality act that companies and organisations need to abide by in the UK. There are 9 different categories:

1. Age
	1. You can't discriminate based on age, unless you've got a valid reason for this being the case (e.g. safety reasons). 
		1. So under-21s can't drive a HGV for certain categories, Bartenders need to be 18+, 
2. Disability
	1. If someone has a mental or physical impairment they should have reasonable adjustments made for them at work and cannot be refused employment for it.
3. Gender Reassignment
	1. It's illegal to treat those who are transgender or those who are transitioning from work, they should be treated as being absent due to illness or injury in these cases.
4. Race
	1. You can't refuse opportunities based on ethnic origin, skin colour, etc.
5. Religion or Belief
	1. Discrimination shouldn't occur to those who have or don't have a certain belief.
6. Sex/Gender
	1. You can't discriminate someone from opportunities if they are a man or a woman.
	2. This can be waived if the job demands gender as being legitimate to the work.
		1. So Care workers, people in changing rooms, women who suffer domestic abuse, Catholic priests must be men, etc.
7. Sexual Orientation
	1. People shouldn't be excluded for being straight, bi, or gay. Or anything in else not covered by the act.
8. Pregnancy/Maternity
	1. Employers need to take into account pregnancy and maternity and give maternity leave.
9. Marriage and Civil Partnership
	1. If someone is married, they shouldn't be discriminated against.

## Concepts of Discrimination/Equality Act:

- **Direct Discrimination** - When a person is treated less favourably because of an actual or perceived characteristic. 
- **Discrimination by Association** - When someone is treated less favourably because of an association with someone who has a protected characteristic.
	- Doesn't apply to taking care of partner or children. But say, you can't get time off to take care of someone disabled. And allowing others time off would be discrimination.
- **Indirect Discrimination** - When a neutral practice puts those with a protected characteristic at a disadvantage that cannot be justified.
- **Perception Discrimination** - When someone discriminates against a person who they believe has a protected characteristic even if they don't.
- **Harassment** - Unwanted behaviour that has the purpose of violating another person's dignity and creates a degrading, hostile or intimidating environment.
- **Victimisation** - Victimisation occurs when someone is treated badly because they have made or supported a complaint or raised a grievance under the Equality Act, or it is suspected that they have done so.
- **Reasonable Adjustments** - Those with protected characteristics have a right to ask for reasonable adjustments being made in the workplace to remove barriers and make them accessible.
	- Adjustments that have little or no cost should be readily implemented. Depending on the size and nature of the organisation.
- **Discrimination Arising from Disability** - If someone has been sick for many days due to a disability. They should not be dismissed for this. Even if a regular worker may have been
	- Unless there is no chance that the worker will be able to return to work and there are no reasonable adjustments that could be made to support the return to work.
- **Positive Action** - Positive action means that employers can take steps to encourage people from those with different needs, a past record of disadvantage or low participation to apply for jobs.
- **Genuine Occupational Requirement** - If an employer can show that they need someone from a particular protected characteristic to do a job.
	- Female workers to work with domestic abuse victims for example.

# Neurodiversity:

Neurodiversity refers to different conditions that should be respected like any other human condition, these include:

- Dyslexia
- Autism
- ADHD
- Dyspraxia
- Tourette's
- Other neurological conditions

It is estimated that 10% of the population are neurodivergent. This untapped pool of talent has many positive benefits to an organisation, such as creativity and lateral thinking.

Organisations should do the following to enable neurodiverse workers and candidates:

1. Offer training to line managers to support this.
2. Encourage neurodiverse workers to speak with management to settle reasonable adjustments.
3. Develop recruitment methods and processes that accommodate the needs of a neurodiverse candidate.
4. Develop clear communication protocols with neurodiverse candidates.
5. Make reasonable adjustments.

You can't disclose these details to other people without permission, so you should discuss needs prior to a role starting.

# Gender Pay Reporting:

**Equal Pay:** All employers must pay men and women equally for the same work or work of equal value.

**Gender Pay Gap Reporting:** Employers with 250+ employees must annually publish six specific figures showing the average pay difference between male and female staff across the whole organisation. This is about transparency, not enforcement — there's no legal requirement to close the gap, only to report it.

The gender pay gap exists for a few reasons:

1. More men in senior roles. These roles earn more which drags up the male average.
2. Women may be less confident in seeking pay increases or negotiation.
3. Women are more likely to undertake caring responsibilities. 
4. Women are more likely to work part time.
5. Women are more likely to work in low-paying roles
6. Women are overrepresented in certain fields and under represented in others.

The gender pay gap can be remediated by:
1. Supporting employees and their family
2. Encouraging women to seek higher level opportunities.
3. Seek to understand why certain areas attract more men than women. And how more women can be attracted.
4. Create an inclusive environment.
5. Review recruitment policies and practices to remove bias.
6. Ensure pay policies and practices eliminate gender bias.
