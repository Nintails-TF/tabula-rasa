# What is Data Protection?

Data protection is governed by various laws, but the UK General Data Protection Regulation (UK GDPR) is one of them. The goal of the UK-GDPR is to protect people and organisations by reducing risks that can arise by the deliberate or accidental misuse of data and personal information.

***

# Different Categories of Personal Data:

## Personal Data:

Personal Data refers to any information or identifier that relates directly or indirectly to a person. Within the context of the OU this refers to a staff number, a student's Personal Identifier (PI)m and email address. That could be used to identify a living person.

This information is protected to make sure that it isn't stolen, accessed, changed or disclosed. Without a valid reason.
## Special Category Data:

Certain forms of data are more protected that others, the processing of this data is more secure, it includes but is not limited to:

- Racial or ethnic origin.
- Political opinions.
- Religious or philosophical beliefs.
- Trade union membership.
- Genetic data.
- Biometric data used to identify that person e.g., a fingerprint.
- Health and disability data.
- Data concerning someone's sex life or sexual orientation.
- Data relating to criminal convictions or offences.
## Highly Confidential Personal Data:

Any data that falls under personal or special is highly confidential

# Organisational compliance with the UK-GDPR:

All organisations are held accountable by an Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) into complying with the 7 principles of the UK-GDPR, this is done annually.

1. Lawfulness, Fairness & Transparency
	1. You have to be clear and open with how you'll use personal data, and have a lawful reason for collecting and using personal data.
2. Purpose Limitation
	1. Be clear what purpose you are processing data for. 
		1. This needs to be recorded as part of documentation.
3. Data Minimisation
	1. Ensure that the personal data you are processing is needed for the work and role.
4. Accuracy
	1. Ensure all personal data you hold is correct and not misleading, either correct or delete it if it is misleading.
5. Retention, Storage, and Deletion
	1. Personal data should not be kept longer than you need it, you should review and delete data regularly.
		1. This should be done annually, documents should have a date for review.
6. Integrity and Confidentiality 
	1. Data must be kept secure by appropriate technical and organisational measures.
		1. Abiding by the CIA triad.
7. Accountability and Governance
	1. Individuals and organisations are accountable to upholding the UK GDPR.

As per difference by the UK/EU - The UK generally collects more data and is more willing to hold data of citizens and use it against them than other EU countries, the UK GDPR has exemptions written into law because of this

# Rights of People under the UK-GDPR:

Time limits apply when people exercise their rights and requests. These should be referred to an information rights team to ensure that these time limits are met.

1. The right to be informed.
	1. Privacy policies or information can be viewed to described how data is used and shared with.
2. Automated Decision making and profiling
	1. An individual can request as a result of the UK-GDPR to get information about an automated process or to challenge an automated decision.
3. The right to object
	1. An individual can make an objection
4. The right to access
	1. An individual can make a SAR - which occurs within one month and can be used to request a copy of data of the individual.
5. The right to data portability
	1. Data can be transferred and replicated across environments for ease of use in IT systems.
6. The right of rectification
	1. Inaccurate data should be modified and fixed within one month.
7. The right of Erasure
	1. Our personal data should be deleted when asked by and should be complied within one month. 
	2. Data erasure can be stopped if one of the following reasons apply.
		1. To exercise the right of freedom of expression and information;
		2. To comply with a legal obligation;
		3. For the performance of a task carried out in the public interest or in the exercise of official authority;
		4. For archiving purposes in the public interest, scientific research, historical research or statistical purposes where erasure is likely to render impossible or seriously impair the achievement of that processing; or
		5. For the establishment, exercise or defence of legal claims.
		6. Medical data is also difficult to erase.
8. The right to restrict processing
	1. Data processing can be suspended even if data is retained.

# Data Protection by Design and Default:

When accessing data for the first time a DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment) is done. Failure to complete a DIPA, means that a company can be fined up to £17.5M or 4% of last years global annual turnover, whatever is highest.

A lot of this applies with mergers, when a merger occurs with a third party. Both parties need to check what information they are sending to each other by completing IAR (Information Asset Register).
