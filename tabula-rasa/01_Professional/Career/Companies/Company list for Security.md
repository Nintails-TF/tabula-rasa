# Tier One: Security is Existential:

**These organisations cannot survive a serious breach, or their entire value proposition depends on being secure.**

- Quantitative trading firms and high-frequency traders. Their algorithms are worth billions. A breach that exposes strategies is game over. They hire exceptional people and pay accordingly.
- Cryptocurrency exchanges and custodians. Assets are bearer instruments. Theft is irreversible. The firms that still exist have survived because they take security seriously. The ones that didn't are gone.
- Cloud infrastructure providers. AWS, Azure, GCP. Customer trust is the product. A major breach would be an existential crisis. They have some of the most sophisticated security programmes anywhere.
- **Defence and intelligence contractors handling classified work**: The threat model is nation-states. The requirements are non-negotiable. Security culture tends to be genuine, not performative.
- **Password managers and identity providers**: Their entire business is "trust us with your secrets." A breach destroys the company. They have to be good.

# Tier Two: Security is a Serious Business Concern.

**Failure is extremely costly and leadership understands this.**

- **Major banks and financial institutions**: Regulated, targeted, holding assets. Security investment is substantial and generally genuine.
- **Payment processors and card networks**: They sit at the centre of financial flows. Breaches affect the entire ecosystem.
- **Large technology companies with significant data holdings**: Not all are equal, but companies like Apple, Google, Microsoft have sophisticated programmes because they're high-value targets with reputations to protect.
- **Critical infrastructure operators—energy, grid, pipelines**: Post-Colonial Pipeline, post-Ukraine attacks, there's genuine attention here. The best operators understand that failures have physical consequences.

# Tier Three: Security is Important but Competes with other Priorities.

**Investment is real but variable. You need to evaluate specific organisations.**

- **Large enterprises post-breach**: Companies that have been burned often transform. The institutional memory persists for years. These can be excellent environments because leadership finally gets it.
- **Insurance companies**: They hold sensitive data and increasingly understand cyber risk because they're pricing it for others.
- **Telecommunications**: Varies significantly by company, but the major carriers are serious targets and some invest accordingly.
- **Large private healthcare systems and insurers:** The American giants—UnitedHealth, Anthem, Kaiser—have programmes that look more like financial services. They have money, face liability, and can lose customers.
- **Pharmaceuticals with valuable IP**: Drug development is enormously expensive. Protecting research from nation-state theft is a recognised priority at major firms.

# Tier Four: Security Exists but is Often Subordinate.

**Compliance drives most investment. Genuine capability is hit or miss.**

- **Government agencies**: Highly variable. Some defence-adjacent agencies are serious. Many civilian agencies are bureaucratic nightmares—compliance-heavy, capability-light, constrained by procurement and hiring rules.
- **Large retailers**: Unless they've been breached, security often loses to other priorities. Target improved dramatically post-breach. Others haven't had their wake-up call.
- **Manufacturing**: Operational technology concerns are driving more investment, but many manufacturers still treat security as IT's problem.
- **Professional services**: law firms, consultancies. They hold sensitive client data but historically underinvest. Improving as clients demand security assurances, but maturity varies enormously.
- **Airlines and transportation**: Regulated, some genuine concerns, but often running legacy systems with constrained budgets.

# **Tier Five: Security is Primarily theatre.**

**Compliance checkbox exercises. Cigar butt territory.**

- **Small and mid-size enterprises generally**: They buy the cheapest MSSP, do the minimum for compliance, and hope they're not targeted. Resources aren't there for genuine security.
- **Public healthcare**: NHS and equivalents globally. Perpetually underfunded, always prioritising patient care over security, running legacy systems, fragmented governance. The data is sensitive but the investment isn't there.
- **Education:** Universities have fascinating threat profiles—research IP, student data, nation-state interest—but security is dramatically underfunded almost everywhere. IT security teams are small, under-resourced, and fighting losing battles.
- **Local and regional government**: Ransomware's favourite targets. Minimal budgets, legacy systems, no security expertise, political leadership that doesn't understand the risk until it's too late.
- **Non-profits and NGOs**: Some handle sensitive data—human rights organisations, for instance—but resources rarely exist for proper security.
- **Hospitality. Hotels, restaurants, entertainment**: Security is an afterthought unless payment card compliance forces attention.
# **Tier Six: Provide compliance for Tier 5:**

**Deliver poor security, for people who want to feel safe or signal safety.**

- **High-volume MSSPs selling commoditised SOC services**: The ticket farms. You're a cost to be minimised, measured on throughput, and replaced when burned out.
- **Compliance mills**: Firms that exist to produce audit documentation. You'll learn frameworks and checkbox-filling, not security.
- **Penetration testing factories**: Volume shops churning cookie-cutter assessments with inadequate time. The work sounds impressive but the development is minimal.

***
# **Caveats And nuances.**

1. Within any tier, specific teams and leaders matter enormously. A good security leader in a tier-four organisation can create an excellent environment. A bad leader in tier one can make it miserable.
2. Size and resources matter but aren't determinative. A well-funded startup with security-conscious founders might be better than a large enterprise with dysfunctional security politics.
3. Your role within the organisation matters. Being on the red team at a tier-three company might be better than being in compliance at a tier-one firm.
4. The threat landscape an organisation faces teaches you different things. Nation-state threats teach different skills than organised crime, which teaches different skills than opportunistic attackers.
