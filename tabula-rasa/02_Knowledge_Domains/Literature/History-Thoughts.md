# History

## Ancient World

**Greek:**
- **Homer** (c. 8th century BCE) — Iliad, Odyssey. The starting point of Western literature. War, homecoming, heroism, what it means to be mortal.
- **Aeschylus** (c. 525–456 BCE) — The Oresteia. Earliest of the three great tragedians. Justice, fate, the transition from vengeance to law.
- **Sophocles** (c. 496–406 BCE) — Oedipus Rex, Antigone. Fate, human limitation, moral conflict.
- **Euripides** (c. 480–406 BCE) — Medea, The Bacchae. More psychological, questions traditional values.
- **Thucydides** (c. 460–400 BCE) — History of the Peloponnesian War. Still the sharpest analysis of power, war, and political decay.
- **Plato** (c. 428–348 BCE) — Apology, Republic, Symposium. Sets the terms for Western philosophy.
- **Aristotle** (384–322 BCE) — Nicomachean Ethics, Politics, Rhetoric. More practical than Plato. Virtue ethics maps well onto systematic self-cultivation.

**Roman:**
- **Cicero** (106–43 BCE) — Rhetoric, republican politics, practical philosophy. The Romans' great synthesizer of Greek thought.
- **Virgil** (70–19 BCE) — Aeneid. Rome's founding myth, duty versus desire.
- **Seneca** (c. 4 BCE–65 CE) — Letters, essays. Stoic philosophy made practical and accessible.
- **Epictetus** (c. 50–135 CE) — Discourses, Enchiridion. Stoic discipline, focusing on what you control.
- **Marcus Aurelius** (121–180 CE) — Meditations. A journal of self-improvement, discipline, equanimity.

**Indian:**
- **Upanishads** (c. 800–200 BCE) — Metaphysical foundations, nature of self and reality.
- **Bhagavad Gita** (c. 200 BCE–200 CE) — Action, duty, detachment. How to act without being enslaved by outcomes.

**Chinese:**
- **Laozi** (c. 6th century BCE, traditional dating) — Dao De Jing. Yielding, naturalness, effortless action.
- **Confucius** (551–479 BCE) — Analects. Ethics, self-cultivation, social harmony.
- **Sunzi** (c. 5th century BCE) — Art of War. Strategy, applicable beyond warfare.

## Modern World (17th–21st Century)

**Political thought and statecraft:**
- **Machiavelli** (1469–1527) — The Prince. How power actually works, stripped of moralism.
- **Hobbes** (1588–1679) — Leviathan. Social contract, the state as escape from chaos.
- **Locke** (1632–1704) — Two Treatises of Government. Natural rights, consent of the governed.
- **Rousseau** (1712–1778) — The Social Contract. Popular sovereignty, general will.
- **Burke** (1729–1797) — Reflections on the Revolution in France. Conservatism, scepticism of revolutionary change.
- **Franklin** (1706–1790) — Autobiography. Self-improvement, practical wisdom, civic life.
- **Jefferson** (1743–1826) — Declaration of Independence, letters. Natural rights, republican ideals.
- **Madison** (1751–1836) — Federalist Papers. Constitutional design, factions, checks and balances.
- **Hamilton** (1755–1804) — Federalist Papers, economic policy. Strong central government, financial systems.
- **Tocqueville** (1805–1859) — Democracy in America. Still one of the sharpest analyses of American society.
- **Mill** (1806–1873) — On Liberty, Utilitarianism. The harm principle, individual freedom.
- **Lincoln** (1809–1865) — Speeches, letters. Democratic leadership under existential pressure.

**Economics:**
- **Smith** (1723–1790) — Wealth of Nations, Theory of Moral Sentiments. Foundations of political economy.
- **Malthus** (1766–1834) — Essay on the Principle of Population. Population dynamics, resource limits.
- **Ricardo** (1772–1823) — Principles of Political Economy. Trade theory, comparative advantage.
- **Marx** (1818–1883) — Capital, Communist Manifesto. Critique of capitalism, class analysis.
- **Keynes** (1883–1946) — General Theory. Macroeconomics, government's role in markets.
- **Hayek** (1899–1992) — Road to Serfdom. Markets, spontaneous order, critique of central planning.
- **Friedman** (1912–2006) — Capitalism and Freedom. Monetarism, free markets, individual liberty.

**Philosophy:**
- **Descartes** (1596–1650) — Meditations. Modern philosophy begins here, method and doubt.
- **Hume** (1711–1776) — Treatise of Human Nature, Enquiries. Empiricism, scepticism, causation.
- **Kant** (1724–1804) — Critique of Pure Reason, Groundwork. Synthesis of rationalism and empiricism, moral philosophy.
- **Nietzsche** (1844–1900) — Beyond Good and Evil, Genealogy of Morals. Critique of morality, will, meaning after God.
- **William James** (1842–1910) — Pragmatism, Varieties of Religious Experience. Pragmatism, psychology.

**Science:**
- **Newton** (1643–1727) — Principia. Mathematical universe, laws governing nature.
- **Faraday** (1791–1867) — Experimental work on electromagnetism. Foundation for field theory.
- **Darwin** (1809–1882) — On the Origin of Species. Evolution, the mechanism behind life's complexity.
- **Maxwell** (1831–1879) — Electromagnetic theory. Unified electricity, magnetism, and light.
- **Einstein** (1879–1955) — Relativity papers. Reshaped physics, space, time, energy.

**Literature and culture:**
- **Shakespeare** (1564–1616) — Plays, sonnets. Unmatched for understanding human nature through drama.
- **Goethe** (1749–1832) — Faust, Wilhelm Meister. Central to European intellectual culture.
- **Emerson** (1803–1882) — Essays. American transcendentalism, self-reliance.
- **Thoreau** (1817–1862) — Walden, Civil Disobedience. Simplicity, individual conscience.
- **Dostoevsky** (1821–1881) — Crime and Punishment, Brothers Karamazov. Psychology, moral depth, faith and doubt.
- **Tolstoy** (1828–1910) — War and Peace, Anna Karenina. Epic scope, moral searching.
