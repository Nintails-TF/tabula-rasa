# Education and Cyclical Failures of Society

We began with two reading lists representing the Western canon and a modern cross-domain curriculum. A person who genuinely completed either would be extraordinarily rare — deeply humble, hard to manipulate, and equipped to wrestle seriously with the fundamental questions of existence. The value wouldn't be the knowledge itself but the transformation of character the knowledge produces.

This led to the observation that genuine education has almost completely disappeared. The modern university degree certifies bureaucratic compliance, not intellectual formation. The majority of current graduates would fail the entrance examinations of a 19th century college. What was once the transmission of a civilization's accumulated wisdom has become a debt-generating credential machine optimized for throughput.

The degradation followed a traceable arc: mass expansion after WWII, student loan financialization, corporate capture of governance, adjunctification of faculty. Each step individually rational. The accumulated result catastrophic.

We then asked why, even with free access to everything, almost nobody pursues genuine self-education. The conclusion was that the barrier was never information. It was something prior — a disposition that most people simply don't have. We struggled to name it and arrived at a cluster: the tolerance for being lost, genuine hunger for truth rather than stimulation, constitutional seriousness, and above all the ontological courage to risk your current self in the encounter with something greater. The Germans came closest with **Bildung** — self-formation through encounter with greatness.

This raised the uncomfortable structural question of whether mass critical thinking is actually compatible with how modern societies function. Systems depending on compliance work better with populations that are just educated enough to be useful and not educated enough to be dangerous. The capture of education wasn't purely accidental. A pacified credentialed population is a more manageable one.

We then acknowledged the honest counter-argument. Most people don't need philosophical formation. They need a skill and a functional life. The apprenticeship model served this honestly for centuries. The medieval world maintained two tracks without pretending they were the same — genuine universities for the few, genuine craft transmission for the many. Both were destroyed and replaced with a single mass credentialing system that does neither.

The reason this happened was simple and probably inevitable. **Apprenticeships cannot be scaled. Education — even compromised education — can.** Industrial economies needed standardized workers at volume. The degree was the perfect sorting mechanism. The content was always secondary to the certification.

The degradation then followed its own natural logic. People saw others attending university and getting better lives. They copied the visible external form without understanding what generated the value. Universities faced a choice between maintaining standards and serving demand. Serving demand was financially irresistible. The credential retained its social value through inertia long after the substance was gone. Nobody needed to be malicious. Everyone was responding rationally to the incentives in front of them.

This is the same mechanism by which nations fail. Nobody chooses decline. Nobody votes for collapse. A thousand individually rational decisions accumulate silently over generations until the consequences become irreversible — and the people who bear the consequences are never the people who made the decisions. The feedback loop that might correct behaviour is broken entirely.

This brought us to Toynbee — a figure who spent forty years documenting exactly this pattern across twenty-six civilizations and is now almost entirely unknown. His framework maps onto the present with uncomfortable precision:

The **Creative Minority** — genuine innovators, deep professionals, real scientists — generate authentic responses to real challenges. The **Dominant Minority** — corporations, captured institutions, political classes — defend their own position whilst performing the language of the creative minority they replaced. **Universal States and Churches** — totalitarianisms, technocracies, algorithms — impose order and manufacture meaning simultaneously for populations that have lost faith in existing institutions. And the **Schism of the Soul** — the four psychological responses of ordinary people to civilizational decline: archaism, futurism, detachment, and transfiguration.

**Humans are very good at extracting value from systems and very bad at maintaining the conditions that make the systems valuable.**

# Practical Application:

All an individual can do, is to limit the personal damage, and to preserve what matters and has value, even during the decline and destruction.

I think that one day, when I have more money and resources. I'll do the same, I'll protect or record something valuable for myself, so other people can see or access it.
